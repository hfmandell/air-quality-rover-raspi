What is Particulate Matter (PM)?
A small look into these even smaller particles.
Tiny but Mighty
Particulate Matter are named by their largest encompassing size. PM10 includes all particles smaller than 10 micrometers, PM2.5 includes all particles smaller than 2.5 micrometers (and is thus included in PM10), and likewise for PM1.
Sources
Particulate Matter is birthed from many different origins, some anthropogenic and others entirely organic. These sources are further divided into Primary (direct) and Secondary (indirect) sources. Commonly, PM is thought to simply be the 'stuff' that comprises dust and smog. Although it is entirely true that PM includes these particles, it also includes a much more diverse portfolio of sources.
Primary Sources of PM
  • Mining
  • Dust
  • Wildfires
  • Automobile Emissions
  • Construction & Demolition
  • Road Paving
Particulate Matter can also be deposited into the atmosphere via Secondary Sources. When other pollutants in the atmosphere interact, they can produce airborne particulates. It is a vicious cycle of air pollutants creating other air pollutants - and builds a case for the overall reduction of air pollution.
Secondary Sources of PM
  • NH3 (Ammonia)
  • SOx (Sulfur Oxides)
  • NOx (Nitrous Oxides)
  • Volative Organic Compounds (VOCs)
Exposure Standards
Due to the health risks posed by Particulate Matter, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency has created a range of exposure guidelines to aid the public. PM1 is a newer area of focus in environmental chemistry and is less understood compared to its larger counterparts, it does not currently have its own exposure guidelines and is instead contained within the PM2.5 guidelines. Currently, the Annual Average Exposure Standards are:

PM 2.5

12 μg/m3

PM 10

50 μg/m3

The U.S. EPA also creates 24-hour Exposure Standards. These signify the maximum average amount of relative PM that a normal individual should be exposed to over a 24 hour period.

PM 2.5

35 μg/m3

PM 10

150 μg/m3
Taking Precaution
Reduce your exposure to high levels of Particulate Matter by considering the following actions.
  • Stay informed of your local Air Quality Index
    Knowledge is power. Limit your time outdoors when air quality is poor and PM levels are high.
  • Avoid busy roadways
    Spend as little time as possible near areas of high PM emission. Cars and trucks will emit PM directly from their exhaust pipes. The contents of the exhaust will additionally go through atmospheric reactions to form other harmful secondary-source Particulate Matter that can coagulate around trafficked road areas.
  • Limit Exercise in Polluted Areas
    More air is inhaled during activities that require higher cardiovascular expenditure. If one is located in a space with high levels of Particulate Matter, they will inhale a much higher volume of PM than a non-exercising individual. If possible, exercise in a well-ventilated area.
