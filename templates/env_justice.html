Environmental Justice

Racial and Socioeconomic Inequities within Particulate Pollution.

We Do Not All Breathe the Same Air
Pollutant exposure is disproportianate across racial and socioeconomic lines. Lower-income populations and communities of color experience a higher burden from PM emissions due to structural racism.
Emission producing factories, highways, and other polluting infrastructures are commonly placed in these communities rather than in more affluent, whiter neighborhoods. Without a seat at the political, economic, or social table, these communities are not given the platform to argue otherwise and are subjugated to poor air quality.
The soot, dust, and other inhalable particles produced from these facilites force their surrounding communities to endure poor air quality. It is sadly no surpise that this placement leads to higher rates of heart and lung disese for the surrounding constituents.
Lifelong Impacts
Many disadvantages accompany prolonged exposure to poor air. Medical issues can result in students having to miss school or sit out of events and parents needing to stay home from work to recover. Outside of physical ailments, community members experience higher levels of mental health challenges such as anxiety and depression due to knowledge of - but inability to impart change on - their environmental health situation.
Compounded, these difficulties contribute to the creation of cyclical and generational poverty.
